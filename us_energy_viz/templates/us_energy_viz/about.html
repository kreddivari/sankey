About this project

At a time when many people propose transforming the world’s energy system, it is instructive to consider past energy transitions. The history of past U.S. energy transitions can help us understand our potential future pathways.

The U.S. Energy History Visualization project is conducted by members of the University of Chicago’s Center for Robust Decision-making on Climate and Energy Policy (RDCEP), under the direction of Assoc. Professor Liz Moyer, Dept. of the Geophysical Sciences. The research lead on the project is Robert Suits, U. Chicago Dept. of History. Graphics were built by Nathan Matteson, DePaul University College of Computing and Digital Media, and members of U. Chicago’s Research Computation Center, including Milson Munakami, Sergio Elahi and Ramesh Nair. See www.rdcep.org for further information.

The energy history tool consists of an interactive Sankey diagram of U.S. per capita primary energy use from 1800–2017: a mapping of primary energy sources to sectoral uses. Data on sectoral energy use before 1990 was compiled from historical sources including the U.S. Census and the Census Bureau’s Historical Statistics of the United States. Data for individual years after 1990 is provided by the Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL). The manuscript describing the research is currently in preparation (“Energy Transitions in U.S. History”, Suits, Matteson, and Moyer, 2020).

To use the graph, play an animation with the “play” button at bottom left, or drag the timeline slider to control the animation by hand. Black circles along the timeline mark years of especial interest; click these to open up additional information. To more fairly show the energy requirements of the different sectors, the default setting for the animation is to allocate the waste heat from energy generation to those sectors that use electricity. Click the switch at bottom left to turn off this feature. The agricultural sector here represents on-field energy use and does not include embodied energy in fertilizer.

The Sankey uses units of per capita energy use to best show the structure of the energy sector. Per capita energy use rose by ×3 from 1800-2017, but note that the U.S. population also grew by ×60 over this time period. The stacked bar chart at bottom shows the evolution of fuel usage, i.e. the fractional contribution of each fuel to the U.S. energy mix. The U.S. was primarily wood-powered through the late 1890s, primarily coal-powered through the 1930s, and now is powered largely by petroleum (oil) and natural gas.

The visualization tool is in beta; we welcome comments and suggestions sent to rdcep-info@uchicago.edu.
